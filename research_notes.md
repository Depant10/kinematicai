## A1. PEER-REVIEWED RESEARCH

### NDBI Meta-Analyses and Defining Literature

#### Schreibman et al. (2015) — Defining NDBIs
- **Type:** Peer-reviewed (landmark defining paper)
- **URL:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25737021/
- **Year:** 2015
- **Key findings:** Introduced "Naturalistic Developmental Behavioral Interventions" as an umbrella term unifying ESDM, PRT, JASPER, EMT, Project ImPACT, and Incidental Teaching. Identified 13 defining features: use of naturalistic settings, child-initiated teaching episodes, natural contingencies/reinforcement, developmental sequence, shared control, individualized goals, manualized implementation, fidelity measurement. Interventions blend ABA principles with developmental science.
- **Relevance to plan:** Foundational framework — the plan should be built on NDBI principles. Validates child-led, naturalistic approach as evidence-based.
- **Quality / caveats:** Conceptual paper, not an empirical study. Widely cited and accepted.

#### Network Meta-Analysis of Parent-Mediated NDBIs (2024)
- **Type:** Peer-reviewed meta-analysis (32 RCTs)
- **URL:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/ (PMC, 2024)
- **Year:** 2024
- **Key findings:** Compared different NDBI methods across 32 RCTs. PRT showed significant improvements in child social skills, language, and parent fidelity. ESDM showed significant improvements in child language and motor skills. ImPACT was particularly effective for parent implementation fidelity. NDBIs combined with AAC produced larger language improvements for children with minimal speech than NDBIs alone.
- **Relevance to plan:** Supports recommending NDBI techniques for family use. Highlights that AAC + NDBI is better than NDBI alone for minimally verbal children.
- **Quality / caveats:** Tier 1 evidence (network meta-analysis of RCTs). Methodological variability across included studies.

#### Systematic Review of NDBIs — Communication Outcomes
- **Type:** Peer-reviewed systematic review
- **Year:** 2020–2024 (multiple reviews)
- **Key findings:** NDBIs show significant positive effects on communication/language, social engagement, cognition, play, and adaptive behavior. Effects on autism symptomatology and restricted/repetitive behaviors are mixed. Need for larger samples and standardized protocols.
- **Relevance to plan:** Confirms NDBI approach is the strongest evidence base for language intervention in young autistic children.
- **Quality / caveats:** Strong evidence overall but heterogeneity in outcome measures across studies.

### JASPER (Joint Attention, Symbolic Play, Engagement, and Regulation)

#### Kasari et al. — Multiple JASPER RCTs (2006–2022)
- **Type:** Peer-reviewed RCTs (multiple)
- **URL:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/ (various Kasari, Shih, Shire papers)
- **Year:** 2006–2022
- **Key findings:** JASPER targets joint attention and play as precursors to language. Joint engagement mediates improvements in social communication, which then leads to receptive and expressive language gains. RCTs range from pilot (N=15) to larger (N=113 toddlers). Moderate to large effect sizes for play diversity and communicative gestures. Skills generalize to classroom settings and different partners. "Super responders" identified by higher baseline play diversity.
- **Relevance to plan:** Core techniques (following child's lead, joint engagement, symbolic play) directly applicable to home setting. Important: the foundation is play and shared attention, not drilling language forms.
- **Quality / caveats:** Tier 1 evidence. Multiple independent RCTs. Some studies have small samples. Larger confirmatory trials ongoing.

#### Shih et al. (2021) — JASPER Mediation Analysis
- **Type:** Peer-reviewed (secondary analysis)
- **Year:** 2021
- **Key findings:** Empirically validated that joint engagement serves as the mechanism through which JASPER improves joint attention and subsequently language. Confirms the theoretical pathway.
- **Relevance to plan:** Validates prioritizing joint engagement over direct language drilling.
- **Quality / caveats:** Secondary analysis of existing RCT data. Strong mediation evidence.

### Early Start Denver Model (ESDM)

#### Meta-analysis of ESDM (12 studies, N=640)
- **Type:** Peer-reviewed meta-analysis
- **URL:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/ (multiple sources including Waddington et al.)
- **Year:** 2016–2023
- **Key findings:** Significant improvements in language (moderate effect size g=0.408) and cognition. No statistically significant effects on social communication, adaptive behavior, or RRBs. ESDM is an NDBI designed for very young children (12–48 months). Language gains are the most robust finding.
- **Relevance to plan:** Supports naturalistic approach. Important caveat: ESDM alone may not address social communication adequately — needs to be part of a broader approach.
- **Quality / caveats:** Tier 1 evidence. Effect size moderate, not large. Intensity and age at start moderate outcomes.

### Pivotal Response Treatment (PRT)

#### PRT Efficacy Studies
- **Type:** Peer-reviewed RCTs and reviews
- **Year:** 2006–2024
- **Key findings:** PRT targets "pivotal areas" — motivation, self-management, response to multiple cues, social initiations. Significant improvements in functional utterances, vocabulary, social communication, and mean length of utterance. Emphasis on child-led activities and natural reinforcement (child gets what they asked for). Skills generalize across settings. Parents can learn to implement with fidelity. What Works Clearinghouse rates evidence as "small" for communication — more large-scale RCTs needed.
- **Relevance to plan:** Core PRT principles (motivation, natural reinforcement, child choice) are directly implementable by families. Key technique: reinforcing communication attempts naturally.
- **Quality / caveats:** Tier 1–2 evidence. Multiple RCTs but some with small samples. Widely considered evidence-based.

---

### Enhanced Milieu Teaching (EMT)

#### EMT Systematic Reviews and RCTs
- **Type:** Peer-reviewed systematic reviews and RCTs
- **URL:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/ (Kaiser & Roberts work)
- **Year:** 2011–2024
- **Key findings:** EMT has six core strategies: environmental arrangement, responsiveness, modeling, expanding, eliciting, prompting. Effective in increasing vocabulary diversity and spontaneous speech frequency. Caregiver-implemented EMT sometimes shows greater effects on expressive vocabulary than therapist-only delivery. Skills generalize across partners and contexts and maintain over time. Successfully adapted across populations and settings.
- **Relevance to plan:** EMT strategies are highly practical for family implementation. Environmental arrangement, modeling, and expansion are core techniques for the plan.
- **Quality / caveats:** Tier 1 evidence. Strong research base from single-case and group designs. Dosage and child characteristics moderate outcomes.

### Hanen "More Than Words" (MTW)

#### Carter et al. — MTW RCT
- **Type:** Peer-reviewed RCT
- **URL:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21969874/
- **Year:** 2011
- **Key findings:** MTW consistently increases parental self-efficacy and responsiveness. Child communication effects are complex — no significant main effects across all participants. Children with lower baseline object interest showed greater communication gains (moderator effect). Positive trends at follow-up. Telepractice delivery shows promise.
- **Relevance to plan:** MTW techniques (OWL — Observe, Wait, Listen; following child's lead; environmental arrangement) are core to the plan. Important: program may work better for certain profiles. The techniques themselves are evidence-informed even if the packaged program has mixed results.
- **Quality / caveats:** Tier 1 evidence (RCT), but main effect was non-significant. Moderator finding is important. Parental benefit is well-established.

### Gestalt Language Processing (GLP) / Natural Language Acquisition (NLA)

#### Systematic Review of GLP/NLA Evidence (2024)
- **Type:** Peer-reviewed systematic review
- **URL:** https://gestaltlanguageprocessing.org/
- **Year:** 2024
- **Key findings:** A 2024 systematic review found NO published peer-reviewed research studies investigating the effectiveness of therapy based on GLP or NLA. Literature is primarily books, descriptive articles, and case commentaries. Critics argue NLA stages lack standardized criteria and the "abrupt" progression contradicts broader language development research. HOWEVER, the framework is widely endorsed by neurodiversity-affirming clinicians and resonates with autistic adults' lived experiences. The concept of echolalia as functional communication IS well-supported in the literature.
- **Relevance to plan:** Must present GLP/NLA honestly — clinically popular and neurodiversity-affirming but lacking controlled evidence. Echolalia-as-meaningful IS evidence-based. The specific NLA stages are clinical wisdom, not empirically validated.
- **Quality / caveats:** Critical distinction: echolalia research is solid; the specific NLA framework is not empirically tested. Present both views honestly.

#### Blanc, M. (2012) — Natural Language Acquisition on the Autism Spectrum (Book)
- **Type:** Book / clinical framework
- **URL:** https://meaningfulspeech.com/
- **Year:** 2012
- **Key findings:** Describes 6 stages of NLA: Stage 1 (whole gestalts/echolalia) → Stage 2 (mixing/matching chunks) → Stage 3 (single words extracted from gestalts) → Stage 4 (novel two-word combinations) → Stage 5 (complex sentences) → Stage 6 (grammatical refinement). Framework positions echolalia as Stage 1 of a natural language acquisition process, not as deviant behavior. Informed by longitudinal clinical observation.
- **Relevance to plan:** If the child uses echolalia/scripting, the NLA framework provides a useful clinical lens. Helps family understand echolalia as meaningful.
- **Quality / caveats:** Tier 4 (clinical wisdom, not peer-reviewed evidence). Widely adopted clinically despite evidence gap.

### Preposition Acquisition in Autism

#### Spatial Language Studies in ASD
- **Type:** Peer-reviewed studies
- **URL:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/ (multiple)
- **Year:** 2010–2023
- **Key findings:** Autistic children show "peaks and troughs" in spatial language — may master early prepositions (in, on, under) similarly to neurotypical peers, but have persistent difficulties with later-acquired terms (near/far, behind, between, left/right). Visuospatial skills are often a strength but mapping those abilities to linguistic labels is the challenge (a language-mapping problem, not a spatial cognition problem). Caregiver spatial language input during play is a strong predictor of child spatial language growth. Suggests delay rather than fundamental inability.
- **Relevance to plan:** Directly relevant. Teaching sequence should follow developmental order (in → on → under → behind → between → beside). Emphasize concrete, physical demonstrations. Caregiver modeling during natural play is evidence-supported.
- **Quality / caveats:** Tier 2 evidence. Limited autism-specific preposition intervention studies; most evidence extrapolated from typical development and general autism language research.

### Verb Learning in Autism

#### Verb Deficit Literature
- **Type:** Peer-reviewed studies
- **URL:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/ (multiple), Frontiers in Psychology
- **Year:** 2005–2023
- **Key findings:** Verbs are harder than nouns for many autistic children due to: (1) verbs are more abstract and contextual than object labels, (2) verb learning relies on social cues (eye gaze, shared attention) that autistic children may process differently, (3) a subgroup shows morphosyntactic deficits — omitting tense markers, struggling with auxiliaries (overlaps with SLI/DLD profile), (4) heterogeneity — many autistic individuals do NOT show verb deficits. "Follow-in" input (narrating what the child is currently doing) significantly supports verb acquisition. Explicit instruction can help when incidental learning is insufficient.
- **Relevance to plan:** Verbs need active, embodied teaching — connect words to actions in the moment. Use "follow-in" commenting. Don't over-focus on nouns at the expense of verbs. Consider explicit teaching if naturalistic modeling isn't sufficient.
- **Quality / caveats:** Tier 2 evidence. Research identifies the problem clearly but intervention studies specifically for verb acquisition in autism are limited. Strategies extrapolated from broader language intervention research.

### Alexithymia in Autism

#### Prevalence and Intervention Literature
- **Type:** Peer-reviewed studies and reviews
- **URL:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/ (multiple)
- **Year:** 2013–2024
- **Key findings:** 50–85% of autistic individuals experience alexithymia (vs 5–13% general population). Stable over time. The "alexithymia hypothesis" proposes that emotion recognition difficulties attributed to autism are actually driven by alexithymia specifically. Alexithymia exacerbates anxiety, depression, and social difficulties. Interventions focus on: visual emotion supports (cards, charts), body awareness (connecting sensations to emotions), adapted CBT, social stories, music therapy as nonverbal expression channel.
- **Relevance to plan:** Directly relevant for this child. Build emotional vocabulary alongside general expressive language. Use body-sensation → emotion mapping. Visual supports for internal states. Don't demand verbal emotional expression before the child has the tools.
- **Quality / caveats:** Tier 2 evidence. Prevalence well-established. Intervention evidence is promising but limited in autism-specific controlled trials.

### AAC and Speech Development

#### Systematic Reviews of AAC in Autism
- **Type:** Peer-reviewed systematic reviews
- **Year:** 2014–2024
- **Key findings:** Extensive evidence that AAC does NOT delay or impede speech development. Many AAC users show improvements in spoken language. AAC reduces frustration and increases social engagement. Users become "total communicators" using speech, gestures, and devices interchangeably. Experts recommend introducing AAC as early as possible — waiting to see if speech develops causes unnecessary delay. Consensus: the goal is functional communication by any means.
- **Relevance to plan:** Critical to include. Families and extended family may resist AAC ("it'll stop him from talking"). The evidence clearly says the opposite. Low-tech AAC (choice boards, communication boards) should be introduced as part of the plan.
- **Quality / caveats:** Tier 1–2 evidence. Consistent across multiple reviews. This is settled science.

---

### Parent-Mediated Intervention Meta-Analyses

#### Systematic Reviews 2020–2024
- **Type:** Peer-reviewed meta-analyses
- **Year:** 2020–2024
- **Key findings:** PMIs show moderate effects on social interaction and positive behaviors. Effective at reducing disruptive behaviors. Mixed results for language/communication specifically (some show small-moderate improvements, others non-significant on clinician-rated measures). Consistently improve parental knowledge and competence. May help alleviate parental stress. Study quality varies; need for more rigorous large-scale trials.
- **Relevance to plan:** Important honest framing: parent-mediated support works, especially for building interaction quality and parental skills. Language outcomes specifically may require professional involvement for optimal results. Home strategies complement, don't replace, professional support.
- **Quality / caveats:** Tier 1 evidence base but with mixed findings on language specifically. Honest about limitations.

---

### Screen Time and Language Development

#### Screen Time Research
- **Type:** Peer-reviewed studies and AAP guidelines
- **Year:** 2016–2024
- **Key findings:** Excessive passive screen time negatively impacts language development. Each minute of screen time displaces "serve-and-return" conversational turns (the primary driver of language acquisition). Children under 3 struggle to learn language from video vs. live interaction. Fast-paced content (e.g., CoComelon-style) may: overstimulate developing brains, affect attention and self-regulation, lead to echolalia of show phrases without meaning comprehension. AAP recommends: no screens under 18 months (except video chat), limited high-quality co-viewed content for 2–5. Co-viewing transforms passive to interactive.
- **Relevance to plan:** Directly relevant — the child has had heavy exposure to algorithm-driven short-form video. Supports the family's decision to remove this. The "lost in own world" pattern may be partly screen-related. Replacement should be interactive engagement, not just removing the screens and leaving a void.
- **Quality / caveats:** Tier 2–3 evidence for language effects. AAP recommendations are well-established. Specific research on "algorithm-driven short-form video" is newer and less robust, but the general screen time evidence is strong.

## A2. CLINICAL GUIDELINES

---

### ASHA (American Speech-Language-Hearing Association)
- **Type:** Clinical guideline / Practice Portal
- **URL:** https://www.asha.org/practice-portal/clinical-topics/autism/
- **Year:** Continuously updated
- **Key findings:** Advocates evidence-based practice integrating clinical expertise, research, and family values. Recommends: AAC, behavioral interventions (PRT, FCT, DTT), naturalistic interventions, visual supports, social/peer-mediated interventions, parent-implemented interventions. Emphasizes individualized care, neurodiversity-affirming practice, comprehensive multidisciplinary assessment, collaborative practice across environments.
- **Relevance to plan:** Authoritative reference for intervention selection. Validates NDBI + AAC + visual supports approach.
- **Quality / caveats:** Tier 3 (clinical guideline from professional body). High authority.

### NICE Guidelines (UK) — CG170
- **Type:** Clinical guideline
- **URL:** https://www.nice.org.uk/guidance/cg170
- **Year:** 2013, updated
- **Key findings:** Recommends social-communication interventions using play-based strategies with parents/carers/teachers. Goals: increase joint attention, engagement, reciprocal communication. Adjust to developmental level. Increase sensitivity/responsiveness of communication partners. SLP should be core team member. Explicitly states: do NOT use neurofeedback or auditory integration training for speech/language.
- **Relevance to plan:** Validates play-based, partner-focused approach. Useful "what not to do" guidance.
- **Quality / caveats:** Tier 3 (national guideline). UK-specific but globally referenced.

### AAP (American Academy of Pediatrics)
- **Type:** Clinical guideline
- **URL:** https://www.aap.org/autism
- **Year:** 2020, updated
- **Key findings:** Don't wait for diagnosis to begin intervention. Screen at 18 and 24 months. Support should be individualized, strength-based, goal-oriented. Emphasizes Early Intervention (0–3) and parent training.
- **Relevance to plan:** Validates urgency of starting support. Emphasizes not waiting.
- **Quality / caveats:** Tier 3. US-specific but globally relevant.

### RCSLT (Royal College of Speech and Language Therapists, UK)
- **Type:** Clinical guideline
- **URL:** https://www.rcslt.org/
- **Year:** Ongoing
- **Key findings:** Emphasizes evidence-based practice, shared decision-making, tailoring support to individual needs and lived experiences. Complements NICE guidance.
- **Relevance to plan:** Supports person-centered, evidence-based approach.
- **Quality / caveats:** Tier 3.

### RCI (Rehabilitation Council of India) and ISHA
- **Type:** Regulatory body / professional association
- **URL:** https://rehabcouncil.nic.in/ and https://ishaindia.org.in/
- **Year:** Ongoing
- **Key findings:** RCI is statutory body regulating rehabilitation professionals. Mandatory CRR registration for legal practice. ISHA promotes professional standards, clinic accreditation, and provides "Locate Professional" tools. Families should verify CRR number. Multiple ISHA-accredited clinics offer teletherapy.
- **Relevance to plan:** Critical for India-specific guidance. Family needs to verify any professional is RCI-registered.
- **Quality / caveats:** Tier 3 (regulatory). Authoritative for Indian context.

## A3. NEURODIVERSITY-AFFIRMING PRACTICE

---

### Double Empathy Problem (Milton, 2012)
- **Type:** Peer-reviewed theoretical paper + subsequent empirical work
- **URL:** https://doi.org/10.1080/09687599.2012.710008
- **Year:** 2012 (original), expanded 2018–2024
- **Key findings:** Communication breakdowns between autistic and non-autistic people are mutual and bidirectional — not a one-sided deficit in the autistic person. Autistic people communicate effectively with other autistic people. The problem is a mismatch of communication styles, not a deficit. Implies: intervention should focus on bridging the gap from BOTH sides, not just training the autistic person to conform.
- **Relevance to plan:** Foundational reframing. Family members need to learn the child's communication style, not just train the child to use theirs. Mutual adaptation.
- **Quality / caveats:** Tier 2 (growing empirical support from Crompton et al. 2020 information transfer studies). Theoretically robust.

### ASAN (Autistic Self Advocacy Network) Positions
- **Type:** Advocacy / position statements
- **URL:** https://autisticadvocacy.org/
- **Year:** Various (2012–2024)
- **Key findings:** Strongly opposes indistinguishability as a treatment goal. Communication is a fundamental right — child should use whatever method works best. Critiques ABA for pursuing normalization. Advocates for self-determination, meaningful goals, and accommodations over conformity. Suppressing stimming leads to shame, mental health harm, and burnout.
- **Relevance to plan:** Critical ethical framing. Plan goals should be communication and connection, NOT looking non-autistic. Stimming should not be suppressed. Eye contact should not be forced.
- **Quality / caveats:** Tier 5 (advocacy/lived experience). Not empirical evidence but represents the community the plan is meant to serve.

### Critiques of Compliance-Based ABA
- **Type:** Peer-reviewed, advocacy, and practitioner literature
- **Year:** 2018–2024
- **Key findings:** Traditional ABA criticized for: prioritizing normalization over wellbeing, undermining autonomy, teaching compliance over self-advocacy (increasing vulnerability), focusing on behavior elimination rather than understanding communication function, causing documented trauma and negative mental health outcomes in autistic adults. Modern "neurodiversity-affirming ABA" exists but is contested. Neurodiversity-affirming SLPs practice assent-based therapy, respect "no" (verbal or body language), develop goals collaboratively.
- **Relevance to plan:** Plan should explicitly avoid compliance-based approaches. Use assent-based engagement. Stop when child signals they're done. Natural reinforcement, not extrinsic rewards for compliance.
- **Quality / caveats:** Tier 2–5 (mix of peer-reviewed critique, practitioner articles, and lived-experience accounts). The harms of traditional ABA are increasingly documented.

### Barry Prizant — SCERTS Model and "Uniquely Human"
- **Type:** Book / clinical framework / peer-reviewed
- **URL:** https://uniquelyhuman.com/
- **Year:** 2006 (SCERTS), 2015 (Uniquely Human book)
- **Key findings:** SCERTS = Social Communication, Emotional Regulation, Transactional Support. All behavior is communication — understand the "why" instead of extinguishing. Focus on emotional regulation as foundation for learning. Create safe environments where the child feels understood. Celebrates neurodiversity. Family-centered, flexible framework integrated into natural environments.
- **Relevance to plan:** Core philosophy. Aligns with NDBI and neurodiversity-affirming values. "All behavior is communication" should be a guiding principle.
- **Quality / caveats:** Tier 2–4 (research-based framework, some peer-reviewed components, some clinical wisdom).

### AssistiveWare Resources
- **Type:** Practitioner / company resource
- **URL:** https://www.assistiveware.com/
- **Year:** Ongoing
- **Key findings:** Provides neurodiversity-affirming AAC resources. Apps: Proloquo, Proloquo2Go, Proloquo4Text, Proloquo Coach. Free resources include core word boards, communication boards, videos. Values diverse communication including echolalia and scripting. Prioritizes agency and emotional safety.
- **Relevance to plan:** Practical resource for AAC implementation. Note: Proloquo apps are iOS-only and paid — need to mention alternatives for Android/budget.
- **Quality / caveats:** Tier 4 (practitioner resource). Well-regarded in the AAC community.
